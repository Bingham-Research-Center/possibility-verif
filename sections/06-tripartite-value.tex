\section{Three-Component Value}\label{sec:tripartite_value}

A reviewer might ask whether all three
components---possibility, ignorance, and conditional necessity---independently
contribute to verification, or whether some are redundant once others
are known.  This section addresses that question directly.  Three
hypothesis test protocols are proposed, each isolating one component and
asking whether it adds information the other two do not provide.  An
affirmative answer for all three justifies the full five-number
scorecard.

%----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Three Hypothesis Tests}\label{sec:three_tests}

The five-number scorecard (Section~\ref{sec:native_scorecard}) can be
used to demonstrate that each of the three named
components---possibility, ignorance, and conditional necessity---independently
contributes verification value.

\paragraph{Test 1: Possibility compatibility ($\alpha^*$, $\delta$).}

\textit{Question:} ``Does the system point at the truth better than
climatology or persistence?''

\textit{How to test:} Compare the forecasting system's resolution gap
($\delta = \alpha^* - \eta$) against the same score for climatological
and persistence baselines.  If the system's $\delta$ is consistently
higher, the possibility component has independent skill.

\textit{Met analogue:} Comparing a model's Brier skill score against
persistence---``Is your resolution better than a na\"ive forecast?''

For SPC outlooks, a system that consistently assigns higher possibility
to the observed category than to the average category will exhibit
positive $\delta$ across the verification sample.  Climatology, by
construction, peaks at the most frequent category and assigns near-zero
possibility to rare outcomes like \spc{HIGH}; a skilful possibilistic
system should outperform this baseline on days when \spc{ENH} or higher
categories verify.

\paragraph{Test 2: Ignorance self-awareness (\ign{}).}

\textit{Question:} ``When the system says it is uncertain, is it
actually performing worse?''

\textit{How to test:} Stratify forecasts by \ign{} (low vs.\ high
ignorance).  Check whether mean $\delta$ is lower in high-ignorance
cases.  If yes, the system's self-reported uncertainty tracks actual
forecast difficulty.

\textit{Met analogue:} The \textbf{spread--skill relationship} in
ensemble forecasting.  Stratify by ensemble spread and check whether
high-spread cases have higher error.  This test asks the same question
about \ign{}: is it an honest uncertainty signal, or just noise?

The implications of this test are decisive for system design.  If \ign{}
does \emph{not} track forecast difficulty, the subnormality signal is
uninformative and normalization is costless---one might as well enforce
$\max(\pi) = 1$ and simplify the framework.  If \ign{} \emph{does}
track difficulty, normalization destroys operationally valuable
information: a forecaster who normalizes away high-\ign{} signals loses
the ability to distinguish confident forecasts from hedged ones.

\paragraph{Test 3: Conditional necessity reliability ($\Nc^*$).}

\textit{Question:} ``When the system says it is sure, is it actually
right more often?''

\textit{How to test:} When $\Nc(c) > \tau$ for some threshold $\tau$,
check whether the conditional hit rate for category $c$ exceeds the base
rate.  If yes, high-necessity forecasts identify genuinely confident
predictions---not just lucky ones.

\textit{Met analogue:} \textbf{Reliability conditional on confidence.}
Meteorologists check this when they ask ``when the ensemble gives
${>}80\%$ chance of severe weather, does it actually verify
${>}80\%$ of the time?''  This test asks: ``when the system says
$\Nc > \tau$, does the truth verify at a rate above climatology?''

Figure~\ref{fig:reliability} illustrates Test~3: conditional hit rate
as a function of the \Nc{} threshold, plotted against the climatological
base rate.  A curve above the base-rate line confirms that high-necessity
forecasts identify genuinely confident predictions.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{reliability_curves.png}
\caption{Reliability of conditional necessity.  The purple curve shows
    the conditional hit rate for the observed category as a function of
    the $\Nc$ threshold $\tau$.  The horizontal dashed line marks the
    climatological base rate ($1/K \approx 0.167$ for $K = 6$ categories).

All three tests require adequate sample sizes to yield statistically
meaningful results.  At pilot scales (${\sim}90$ station-days), the
stratified tests (Tests~2 and~3) will have limited statistical power;
they should be supplemented by illustrative case studies that demonstrate
the qualitative behaviour predicted by each hypothesis.  As a rough
guideline, Test~1 (resolution gap vs.\ baseline) requires at least 50
forecast--observation pairs for a paired-difference bootstrap to detect
moderate effect sizes; Tests~2 and~3 require at least 30 cases per
stratum (high/low \ign{} or above/below the $\Nc$ threshold) to
estimate conditional means with reasonable precision.

\subsection{Three Verification Lanes}\label{sec:three_lanes}

A single metric cannot capture full uncertainty quantification from a
subnormal possibility distribution.  Table~\ref{tab:three_lanes}
summarises the three verification lanes and their respective blind spots.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small
\caption{Three verification lanes and their blind spots.}
\label{tab:three_lanes}
\begin{tabularx}{\columnwidth}{%
    >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.18\columnwidth}XX}
\toprule
Lane & Answers the question & What it misses \\
\midrule
\textbf{Categorical} (POD, FAR, CSI per threshold; HSS)
    & ``Did the mode-based point forecast identify the correct severity
        category?''
    & All distributional information---a correct mode says nothing
        about sharpness, hedging, or confidence. \\
\textbf{Probabilistic} (IG via bridge)
    & ``Was the probabilistic forecast informative?''
    & The bridge is lossy---it compresses the full possibility shape
        into $n{+}1$ probabilities.  Subnormality becomes a single
        ignorance bin. \\
\textbf{Native possibilistic} (scorecard)
    & ``Was the possibility distribution well-shaped, sharp, and
        honest?''
    & No connection to probabilistic skill---a well-shaped distribution
        might still give poor probabilities after conversion. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Together, the three lanes catch failures that any single lane would miss:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item A forecast with correct categorical mode but flat possibility
    (high $\eta$) and poor IG is a broken clock that happens to be
    right---the categorical lane passes but the other two fail.
\item A forecast with great IG but high ignorance and low $\Nc^*$ got
    lucky via the bridge---the raw system was hedging.
\item A forecast with sharp possibility and high $\Nc^*$ but poor IG has
    a bridge problem---the conversion is distorting the signal.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:three_lanes_schematic} provides a visual summary of the
three lanes, showing how the raw possibility distribution feeds into each
verification pathway.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{verification_lanes.png}
\caption{Three-lane verification schematic.  A subnormal possibility
    distribution feeds three parallel verification pathways:
    \textbf{Lane~1} (categorical) evaluates the mode-based point
    forecast via POD, FAR, and CSI at each severity threshold;
    \textbf{Lane~2} (probabilistic) applies the possibility-to-probability
    bridge and evaluates information gain;
    \textbf{Lane~3} (native possibilistic, highlighted in purple)
    evaluates the raw distribution via the five-number scorecard.
    Each lane diagnoses failure modes invisible to
    the others (Table~\ref{tab:three_lanes}).}
\label{fig:three_lanes_schematic}
\end{figure}

In practice, a verification analyst would compile scorecard aggregates
across a test sample, tracking changes between model versions or
seasons.  Figure~\ref{fig:scorecard_table} illustrates this convention
using synthetic data for four model versions.  Metrics are grouped by
verification lane; filled triangles denote statistically significant
change and marker size encodes magnitude, following the scorecard style
used by ECMWF and WMO Lead Centres for NWP inter-comparisons.

\textit{Reading the scorecard at a glance:} a column of large green
triangles means the new version is broadly better; a red triangle amid
green ones flags a trade-off that warrants investigation.  The example
tells a common development story: version~2.0 improved discrimination
but increased ignorance (the system learned to hedge more honestly);
version~3.0 delivered broad gains across all three lanes; version~4.0
pushed discrimination further at the cost of slight reliability
degradation---a trade-off the three-lane view makes immediately visible
but that no single metric could reveal.

\begin{figure*}[b]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{scorecard_table.png}
\caption{Verification scorecard for a hypothetical possibilistic
    forecasting system across three development versions, each compared
    to a v1.0 baseline.  Rows are grouped into three verification lanes:
    native possibilistic (five-number scorecard), probabilistic
    (information gain and its decomposition),
    and categorical (POD, FAR, CSI, HSS).  Filled triangles indicate
    statistically significant improvement (green, upward) or degradation
    (red, downward); open triangles indicate non-significant changes.
    Marker size encodes the magnitude of the change.  Note the
    $\overline{H_\Pi}$ degradation in v2.0: the system became more
    ignorant before improving in subsequent versions---a signature of
    recalibrated uncertainty that the native possibilistic lane detects
    but the probabilistic lane may absorb.}
\label{fig:scorecard_table}
\end{figure*}
