\begin{abstract}
% [PLACEHOLDER: Refine abstract prose after all sections are complete.]

Possibility theory offers a natural framework for encoding epistemic
uncertainty in hazard forecasting, yet no systematic verification
methodology exists for possibilistic predictions.  This paper presents a
self-contained verification framework for subnormal possibility
distributions---distributions whose maximum falls below unity, signaling
that the forecasting system acknowledges gaps in its own knowledge.

We introduce a \emph{five-number scorecard} comprising depth-of-truth
($\alpha^*$), nonspecificity ($\eta$), resolution gap ($\delta$),
possibilistic ignorance ($\ign$), and conditional necessity of truth
($\Nc^*$).  These five statistics map onto three complementary
uncertainty components: possibility (shape quality), ignorance
(subnormality signal), and conditional necessity (dominance of the
observed outcome).  A \emph{possibility-to-probability bridge} converts
subnormal possibilities to probabilities with an explicit ignorance
outcome, enabling information-gain verification without erasing the
subnormality signal.  An \emph{interval log score} extends the
logarithmic scoring rule to interval-valued forecasts derived from the
possibility--necessity duality.  Together, these tools define three
verification lanes---deterministic, probabilistic, and native
possibilistic---each diagnosing failure modes invisible to the others.
All concepts are illustrated with a running example based on the Storm
Prediction Center (SPC) convective outlook categories
(\spc{MRGL}, \spc{SLGT}, \spc{ENH}, \spc{MDT}, \spc{HIGH}).
\end{abstract}

\keywords{possibility theory, forecast verification, subnormal distributions, scoring rules, severe weather}
