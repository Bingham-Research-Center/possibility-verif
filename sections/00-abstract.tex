\begin{abstract}
Possibility theory offers a natural framework for encoding epistemic
uncertainty in hazard forecasting, yet no systematic verification
methodology exists for possibilistic predictions.  This paper presents a
self-contained verification framework for subnormal possibility
distributions---distributions whose maximum falls below unity, signaling
that the forecasting system acknowledges gaps in its own knowledge.
We introduce a \emph{five-number scorecard} comprising depth-of-truth
($\alpha^*$), nonspecificity ($\eta$), resolution gap ($\delta$),
possibilistic ignorance ($\ign$), and conditional necessity of truth
($\Nc^*$), mapping onto three complementary uncertainty components:
possibility, ignorance, and conditional necessity.
A \emph{possibility-to-probability bridge} converts subnormal
possibilities to probabilities with an explicit ignorance outcome,
enabling information-gain verification; an \emph{interval log score}
extends logarithmic scoring to interval-valued forecasts derived from
the possibility--necessity duality.  Together, these tools define three
verification lanes---deterministic, probabilistic, and native
possibilistic---each diagnosing failure modes invisible to the others.
All concepts are illustrated with Storm Prediction Center convective
outlook categories (\spc{NONE} through \spc{HIGH}).
\end{abstract}

\keywords{possibility theory, forecast verification, subnormal distributions, scoring rules, severe weather}
