\section{Native Possibilistic Verification}\label{sec:native_verification}

The possibility-to-probability bridge
(Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}) enables probabilistic verification
of possibilistic forecasts, but the conversion is lossy: it compresses
the full possibility shape into $n{+}1$ probabilities, with the entire
subnormality signal collapsed into a single ignorance bin.  Native
possibilistic verification evaluates the raw distribution directly,
preserving the shape information that the bridge discards.  The metrics
defined below complement the bridge-based lane by diagnosing forecast
quality in possibility space.

All definitions use the notation of Section~\ref{sec:poss_primer}:
$\pi$ denotes the raw (subnormal) possibility distribution,
$m = \max_{\omega \in \Omega} \pi(\omega)$ is the subnormality, and
$\ign = 1 - m$ is ignorance (Eq.~\ref{eq:ignorance}).

%----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Normalization Protocol}\label{sec:norm_protocol}

Some verification quantities require the raw distribution $\pi$ while
others require the normalized form $\pinorm = \pi / m$.  Two
normalization operations serve distinct purposes:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Shape normalization} ($\pi \to \pinorm$): Rescales so
    that $\max(\pinorm) = 1$, isolating the system's relative
    preferences among outcomes.  Used for computing \Nc{}
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:cond_nec}) and the native scorecard
    (Section~\ref{sec:native_scorecard}).
\item \textbf{Possibility-to-probability bridge} ($\pi \to$ probability
    vector): Converts possibilities to probabilities with an explicit
    ignorance outcome (Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}).  Used
    for information-gain verification.
\end{enumerate}

These are not alternatives: normalization operates within the possibility
space; the pignistic bridge converts to the probability space.

Table~\ref{tab:raw_norm} specifies which form each quantity uses.

\textbf{Why this matters:} Getting the raw/normalized distinction wrong
is the single most common error in possibilistic scoring.  Computing
\Nc{} from raw values yields nonsense (it would be negative when
$m < 0.5$).  Computing ignorance from normalized values gives zero by
definition.
The distinction is loosely analogous to evaluating ensemble-mean error
(which uses raw ensemble members) versus a rank histogram (which uses
the normalized rank position of the observation within the ensemble).

\subsection{Five-Number Scorecard}\label{sec:native_scorecard}

Five summary statistics evaluate each forecast--observation pair, where
$c_{\mathrm{obs}}$ denotes the observed convective mode and $K = |\Omega|$
is the number of categories (here, $K = 5$).
Table~\ref{tab:scorecard_defs} lists the definitions; the equations
are given below.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Five-number scorecard definitions.}
\label{tab:scorecard_defs}
\small
\begin{tabular}{clcl}
\toprule
Symbol & Name & Component & Eq.\ \\
\midrule
$\alpha^*$ & Depth-of-truth & Possibility
    & \eqref{eq:alpha_star} \\
$\eta$ & Nonspecificity & Possibility
    & \eqref{eq:ns} \\
$\delta$ & Resolution gap & Possibility
    & \eqref{eq:rg} \\
$\ign$ & Ignorance & Ignorance
    & \eqref{eq:ignorance} \\
$\Nc^*$ & Cond.\ necessity of truth & Necessity
    & \eqref{eq:nc_star} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{equation}
\alpha^* = \pinorm(c_{\mathrm{obs}}) \label{eq:alpha_star}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha^* = 1$ when the truth coincides with the system's peak
category and $\alpha^* = 0$ when the system assigns zero possibility to
the observed outcome.

Following \citet{Klir1995-va}, nonspecificity measures the diffuseness
of the normalized shape:
\begin{equation}
\eta = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{c=1}^{K} \pinorm(c) \label{eq:ns}
\end{equation}
ranging from $1/K$ (sharp) to $1$ (uniform).

\begin{equation}
\delta = \alpha^* - \eta \label{eq:rg}
\end{equation}
Positive $\delta$ indicates the system assigned more possibility to the
truth than to an average category.

\begin{equation}
\ign = 1 - m = 1 - \max_{\omega \in \Omega} \pi(\omega)
\end{equation}
(same as Eq.~\ref{eq:ignorance}).

\begin{equation}
\Nc^* = \Nc(c_{\mathrm{obs}})
    = 1 - \max_{\omega \neq c_{\mathrm{obs}}} \pinorm(\omega)
\label{eq:nc_star}
\end{equation}
where $\Nc^* > 0$ only when the observed category dominates all
alternatives in the normalized distribution.  This is a direct
application of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cond_nec} evaluated at the observation.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\textsf{\color{rationalecolor}Plain-language glosses}}
\smallskip

\noindent
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
\begin{tabularx}{\columnwidth}{%
    >{\columncolor{rationalecolor!10}}c X}
$\alpha^*$ & How much normalized possibility did the system assign to
    what actually happened?  Like POD for the winning bin, but
    continuous. \\
$\eta$ & How spread out was the normalized distribution?  Like ensemble
    spread or sharpness. \\
$\delta$ & Did the system give more support to the truth than to an
    average category?  Like resolution in the Brier decomposition. \\
$\ign$ & How much did the system admit it didn't know?
    $\ign = 0$: fully confident; $\ign = 1$: complete ignorance. \\
$\Nc^*$ & Among scenarios the system covered, was the truth dominant
    enough to rule out alternatives?  A confidence flag. \\
\end{tabularx}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.0}

Aggregates $\overline{\alpha^*}$, $\overline{\eta}$,
$\overline{\delta}$, $\overline{\ign}$, $\overline{\Nc^*}$ are reported
with bootstrap confidence intervals.

Figure~\ref{fig:performance_diagram} provides a diagnostic view of all
five metrics simultaneously, inspired by the performance diagram of
\citet{Roebber2009-rv}.  Each forecast--observation pair maps to a
point whose position encodes depth-of-truth ($\alpha^*$, $y$-axis) and
specificity ($1 - \eta$, $x$-axis); diagonal contour lines mark constant
resolution gap $\delta = \alpha^* - \eta$.  Marker colour encodes
ignorance $\ign$ and marker size encodes conditional necessity $\Nc^*$.

The structural analogy with Roebber's diagram is deliberate.  His
$x$-axis (success ratio) measures forecast precision; here, specificity
$1 - \eta$ plays the same role---how focused was the distribution?  His
$y$-axis (probability of detection) measures whether the forecast
captured what happened; here, $\alpha^*$ asks the same question in
possibility space.  His curved CSI contours measure net quality given
both axes; here, straight $\delta$ contours serve the same purpose
($\delta > 0$ means the system assigned more support to the truth than
to the average category).  Where the diagrams diverge is that
possibilistic forecasts carry two additional uncertainty dimensions---ignorance
$\ign$ (colour) and conditional necessity $\Nc^*$ (marker size)---that
have no analogue in deterministic verification.

\textit{Reading the diagram at a glance:} the upper-right corner is
ideal (sharp and correct); the lower-right is the worst failure mode
(sharp and wrong).  Dark, large markers signal confident, dominant
forecasts; light, small markers signal uncertain, hedged ones.  A
cluster of light markers in the upper left---diffuse but correct---may
indicate a system that captures the truth but lacks the sharpness to be
operationally useful.

\textit{Stratification by observed category.}
Like deterministic accuracy, aggregate possibilistic metrics can be
inflated by trivially correct null forecasts.  If \mode{NULL} days
dominate the sample, a system that is
merely sharp and confident on routine days will exhibit high mean
$\alpha^*$, high mean $\Nc^*$, and low mean $\ign$---regardless of its
skill on rare events.  Figure~\ref{fig:performance_diagram} addresses
this by presenting both the unconditional diagram (panel~a) and the
\mode{CELL}$+$ conditional diagram (panel~b).  The former is needed to
detect false-alarm patterns on null days; the latter exposes the
rare-event population where ignorance and near-miss structures dominate.
For operational dashboards, we recommend reporting scorecard aggregates
separately for null (\mode{NULL}) and eventful (\mode{CELL}$+$) subsets.

\subsection{Interval Log Score}\label{sec:interval_log}

For a threshold event $A_T \subseteq \Omega$---any ``probability of
exceedance'' question, such as $A_T = \{\mode{SUPER},\, \mode{QLCS},\,
\mode{MCS}\}$ for organised convection, or categories above
50\,ppb for ozone, or accumulations exceeding 5\,in.\ for
snowfall---the possibilistic forecast produces an interval $[L, U]$
rather than a point probability.  The bounds derive from the
three-component framework:

\begin{align}
U &= \poss(A_T) = \max_{\omega \in A_T} \pi(\omega)
    \label{eq:U_bound} \\
L &= m \cdot \Nc(A_T)
   = m \cdot \bigl[1 - \max_{\omega \notin A_T} \pinorm(\omega)\bigr]
    \label{eq:L_bound}
\end{align}

The \textit{interval log score} is:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{ILS} = e \cdot \bigl[-\log_2\bigl(\max(U,\, \epsilon)\bigr)\bigr]
    + (1 - e) \cdot \bigl[-\log_2\bigl(\max(1 - L,\, \epsilon)\bigr)\bigr]
\label{eq:interval_log}
\end{equation}
where $e \in \{0, 1\}$ is the event indicator and $\epsilon$ is a small
positive floor (herein $\epsilon = 0.01$, capping the maximum penalty at
$-\log_2(0.01) \approx 6.64$~bits).  When $L = U = p$ (a point
probability), ILS reduces to the standard log score.

\textbf{Motivation.}
Logarithmic scoring is preferred over quadratic (Brier) scoring for rare
severe-weather events because the Brier score barely distinguishes a 0\%
forecast from a 5\% forecast when the event occurs: the squared
penalties are $1.0$ and $0.9025$, respectively.  The log score's
exponential penalty ($-\log_2 0.05 = 4.32$~bits vs.\ $-\log_2 0.0 \to
\infty$) more sharply penalizes overconfident misses, which is precisely
the failure mode of greatest operational concern.

\textbf{Three-component interpretation.}
The forecast assigns possibility $\poss = U$ to the threshold event,
conditional necessity $\Nc = L/m$, and ignorance $\ign = 1 - m$.  The
interval $[L, U] = [m \cdot \Nc,\; \poss]$ brackets the event's
plausibility; width $U - L$ reflects ambiguity between ``merely
possible'' and ``essentially certain.''

\textbf{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item $0 \leq L \leq U \leq 1$ always holds.
\item Under normality ($m = 1$): $L = \nec(A_T)$, $U = \poss(A_T)$.
\item Under extreme subnormality ($m \to 0$): $U \to 0$, so ILS for an
    observed event approaches $-\log_2(\epsilon)$ (maximum penalty);
    $L \to 0$, so ILS for a non-event approaches $-\log_2(1) = 0$ (no
    penalty).  This correctly distinguishes ``no information and the
    event happened'' (costly) from ``no information and nothing
    happened'' (costless).
\end{itemize}

The ILS uses optimistic verification: when the event occurs, the penalty is $-\log_2(U)$, crediting the upper (most generous) bound rather than a point probability.  This means a forecaster can reduce the penalty by widening the interval---assigning higher possibility to more outcomes---without improving the forecast's sharpness.  The width penalty enters implicitly through $\ign$: widening the distribution increases subnormality, which the native scorecard's ignorance metric penalizes.  The ILS is therefore not strictly proper in the classical sense, but the companion $\ign$ metric partially mitigates gaming by exposing the cost of artificial interval inflation.

\textbf{Worked example} (Example~1 from Section~\ref{sec:subnormal},
$\pi = (0.05,\, 0.15,\, 0.70,\allowbreak\, 0.40,\, 0.10)$, threshold
$A_T = \{\mode{SUPER},\allowbreak\, \mode{QLCS},\, \mode{MCS}\}$):
\begin{align*}
m &= 0.70 \\
U &= \max(0.70,\, 0.40,\, 0.10) = 0.70 \\
\pinorm &= (0.071,\; 0.214,\; 1.0,\; 0.571,\; 0.143) \\
\Nc(A_T) &= 1 - \max(0.071,\, 0.214) = 0.786 \\
L &= 0.70 \times 0.786 = 0.550 \quad (L \leq U \;\checkmark) \\
\mathrm{ILS}_{\text{event}} &= -\log_2(0.70) = 0.51\;\text{bits} \\
\mathrm{ILS}_{\text{non-event}}
  &= -\log_2(1 - 0.550) = -\log_2(0.450) = 1.15\;\text{bits}
\end{align*}

forecast scenarios applied to the threshold event
