\section{Possibilistic Verification}\label{sec:native_verification}

The possibility-to-probability bridge
(Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}) enables probabilistic verification
of possibilistic forecasts, but the conversion is lossy: it compresses
the full possibility shape into $n{+}1$ probabilities, with the entire
subnormality signal collapsed into a single ignorance bin.  Native
possibilistic verification evaluates the raw distribution directly,
preserving the shape information that the bridge discards.  The metrics
defined below complement the bridge-based lane by diagnosing forecast
quality in possibility space.

All definitions use the notation of Section~\ref{sec:poss_primer}:
$\pi$ denotes the raw (subnormal) possibility distribution,
$m = \max_{\omega \in \Omega} \pi(\omega)$ is the subnormality, and
$\ign = 1 - m$ is ignorance (Eq.~\ref{eq:ignorance}).

\subsection{Normalisation Protocol}\label{sec:norm_protocol}

Some verification quantities require the raw distribution $\pi$ while
others require the normalised form $\pinorm = \pi / m$.  Two
normalisation operations serve distinct purposes:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Shape normalisation} ($\pi \to \pinorm$): Rescales so
    that $\max(\pinorm) = 1$, isolating the system's relative
    preferences among outcomes.  Used for computing \Nc{}
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:cond_nec}) and the native scorecard
    (Section~\ref{sec:native_scorecard}).
\item \textbf{Possibility-to-probability bridge} ($\pi \to$ probability
    vector): Converts possibilities to probabilities with an explicit
    ignorance outcome (Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}).  Used
    for information-gain verification.
\end{enumerate}

These are not alternatives: normalisation operates within the possibility
space; the pignistic bridge converts to the probability space.

\textbf{Why this matters:} Getting the raw/normalised distinction wrong
is the single most common error in possibilistic scoring.  Computing
$\Nc$ from raw values yields nonsense; computing ignorance from
normalised values gives zero by definition.

\subsection{Five-Number Scorecard}\label{sec:native_scorecard}

Section~\ref{sec:three_component} introduced three diagnostic components
that characterise a forecast \textit{before} the outcome is known:
possibility (plausibility of an event), ignorance (system confidence),
and conditional necessity (dominance among covered scenarios).  Once the
observed category $c_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is available, these components can
be evaluated against the truth.  Five summary statistics result because
the possibility component expands into three metrics: how much support
the truth received ($\alpha^*$), how spread the forecast was ($\eta$),
and whether the shape was net helpful ($\delta$).  Ignorance $\ign$
carries forward unchanged---it is a property of the forecast itself,
included to contextualise the other four metrics, much as sharpness
contextualises reliability in probabilistic verification.
Table~\ref{tab:three_to_five} maps the three pre-event components to
the five post-event metrics.

Five summary statistics evaluate each forecast--observation pair, where
$c_{\mathrm{obs}}$ denotes the observed (verified) SPC category---in
the Day-1 comparison framing, the category from the final SPC
categorical outlook at the point of interest---and $K = |\Omega|$
is the number of categories (here, $K = 6$).
Table~\ref{tab:scorecard_defs} lists the definitions; the equations
are given below.

    & \eqref{eq:rg} \\

\begin{equation}
\alpha^* = \pinorm(c_{\mathrm{obs}}) \label{eq:alpha_star}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha^* = 1$ when the truth coincides with the system's peak
category, $\alpha^* = 0$ when the system assigns zero possibility to the
observed outcome, and intermediate values arise whenever the truth is
plausible but not the system's top pick (e.g., $\alpha^* = 0.267$ for
the Example~1 distribution with $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{ENH}$).

Inspired by the nonspecificity concept of \citet{Klir1995-va}, the
diffuseness of the normalised shape is measured by:
\begin{equation}
\eta = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{c=1}^{K} \pinorm(c) \label{eq:ns}
\end{equation}
ranging from $1/K$ (sharp) to $1$ (uniform).

\begin{equation}
\delta = \alpha^* - \eta \label{eq:rg}
\end{equation}
Positive $\delta$ indicates the system assigned more possibility to the
truth than to an average category.

\begin{equation}
\ign = 1 - m = 1 - \max_{\omega \in \Omega} \pi(\omega)
\end{equation}
(same as Eq.~\ref{eq:ignorance}).

\begin{equation}
\Nc^* = \Nc(c_{\mathrm{obs}})
    = 1 - \max_{\omega \neq c_{\mathrm{obs}}} \pinorm(\omega)
\label{eq:nc_star}
\end{equation}
Structurally, $\Nc^* = 0$ whenever the observed category is not the
unique peak of the normalised distribution (since some alternative then
has $\pinorm = 1$); positive values quantify how strongly the truth
dominates all alternatives.  This is a direct application of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:cond_nec} evaluated at the observation.

\begin{tabularx}{\columnwidth}{%
    >{\columncolor{rationalecolor!10}}c X}
$\boldsymbol{\alpha^*}$ & How much normalised possibility did the system assign to

    continuous.  Range $[0,\, 1]$. \\
$\boldsymbol{\eta}$ & How spread out was the normalised distribution?  Like ensemble

$\boldsymbol{\delta}$ & Did the system give more support to the truth than to an

    Range $[{-}(K{-}1)/K,\; {+}(K{-}1)/K]$; negative when the forecast
    points away from the truth. \\
$\boldsymbol{\ign}$ & How much of the plausibility budget did the system leave
    unassigned?  $\ign = 0$: fully confident; $\ign \to 1$: near-total
    ignorance.  Range $[0,\, 1)$.  The only metric
    that uses the raw distribution; the others use $\pinorm$. \\
$\boldsymbol{\Nc^*}$ & Among scenarios the system covered, was the truth dominant
    enough to rule out alternatives?  Gated: exactly zero unless the
    truth is the unique peak, then continuous in $(0,\, 1]$. \\
\end{tabularx}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.0}

Each metric is well-defined for a single forecast--observation pair.
Overbar notation ($\overline{\alpha^*}$, $\overline{\eta}$, etc.)
denotes aggregation over the verification sample;
the specific aggregation function (mean, median, or other robust
estimator) and uncertainty quantification method are left to the
practitioner.

\textbf{Permutation invariance.}
The five scorecard metrics treat $\Omega$ as an unordered set: they
evaluate whether the forecast pointed at the right category, not whether
it was close in an ordinal sense.  For ordinal domains with well-defined
distances, a distance-sensitive score (via an inter-category cost
matrix) should supplement the scorecard.

Figure~\ref{fig:performance_diagram} provides a diagnostic view of the
five scorecard metrics, inspired by \citet{Roebber2009-rv}.  Hexagonal
bins aggregate forecast--observation pairs by specificity $1 - \eta$
($x$-axis) and depth-of-truth $\alpha^*$ ($y$-axis).  Bin colour
encodes mean ignorance $\ign$: dark bins are confident, pale bins
uncertain.  Hyperbolic contours mark constant joint skill
$S = \alpha^* \times (1 - \eta)$; the dashed diagonal marks
$\delta = 0$.  The green trajectory connects category-conditional
means, tracing how the system degrades from \spc{NONE} (easy, upper
right) to \spc{HIGH} (rare, lower left), with point size proportional
to sample count.

The analogy with Roebber's diagram is deliberate: specificity plays the
role of success ratio (how focused was the distribution?), $\alpha^*$
plays the role of probability of detection (did the forecast capture
truth?), and the $S = \alpha^* \times (1 - \eta)$ hyperbolas play the
role of CSI contours---both are products of the two axes, so iso-lines
of constant joint skill are hyperbolas relating the two axes just as CSI
iso-lines relate success ratio to POD.  The key addition is ignorance $\ign$, encoded
as bin colour, which has no deterministic analogue.

\textit{Reading the diagram:} upper right is ideal (sharp and truthful);
lower right is the worst failure mode (sharp and wrong).  Above the
$\delta = 0$ diagonal, the forecast discriminated; below it, the truth
received less support than the categorical average.  The trajectory's
slope reveals where skill degrades fastest---a steep drop between
adjacent categories signals the system's breaking point.

Both axes of Figure~\ref{fig:performance_diagram} operate on the
normalised shape $\pi'$: two forecasts with identical shapes but
different commitment levels ($\ign = 0.05$ vs.\ $\ign = 0.50$) land at
the same position.  Commitment is visible only through bin colour---a
weaker perceptual channel.
Figure~\ref{fig:commitment_diagram} promotes commitment to a primary
axis: $x = \pi_{\max} = 1 - \ign$ (how much of the unit interval the
forecaster used) versus $y = \delta = \alpha^* - \eta$ (net
discrimination).  These axes are orthogonal---$\pi_{\max}$ is the raw
\emph{scale} of $\pi$; $\delta$ is the \emph{shape quality} of $\pi'$.

\textit{Reading the commitment diagram:} upper right is committed and
discriminating (ideal); lower right is overconfident and wrong; upper
left is the cautious forecast that got the shape right.  Below the
$\delta = 0$ line, the forecast failed to discriminate.  Scenario~C
(sharp-wrong) sits squarely in the overconfident quadrant; Scenario~B
(hedged-correct) sits in the cautious-discriminating region---a
distinction invisible in the shape-quality diagram.
