\section{Worked Examples}\label{sec:worked_examples}

The preceding sections developed three verification lanes---the native
scorecard, the possibility-to-probability bridge with information gain,
and the interval log score---in the abstract.  This section applies all
three to a concrete trio of stylized convective mode scenarios.  Each
scenario is designed to isolate a different diagnostic situation: a sharp
forecast that verifies correctly, a hedged forecast that also verifies
correctly, and a sharp forecast that misses badly.  Walking through the
full scorecard, gauge visualization, bridge computation, and ILS
calculation for each case demonstrates how the metrics complement one
another and how subnormality propagates through every stage of the
framework.

%----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Scenario Setup}\label{sec:scenario_setup}

Three stylized possibilistic convective mode forecasts are defined, each
representing a distinct forecasting situation.  All distributions are
defined over $\Omega = \{\mode{NULL},\, \mode{CELL},\, \mode{SUPER},\,
\mode{QLCS},\, \mode{MCS}\}$ with $K = 5$.  We consider hypothetical
possibilistic convective mode forecasts verified against the observed
dominant storm mode at a given point.

\paragraph{Scenario A: Sharp-correct \mode{SUPER} forecast.}

A classic supercell environment is in place: 0--6~km bulk shear exceeds
40~kt, surface-based CAPE surpasses 2000~J~kg$^{-1}$, and discrete
initiation is expected along a well-defined dryline.  The ingredients for
supercellular convection are co-located and unambiguous, so the system
confidently assigns high possibility to \mode{SUPER} while leaving only
token values for neighbouring categories.  Subnormality is low
($m = 0.85$, $\ign = 0.15$): the system is nearly fully committed.

\[
\pi_A(\omega) = \begin{cases}
0.00 & \omega = \mode{NULL}  \\
0.05 & \omega = \mode{CELL}  \\
0.85 & \omega = \mode{SUPER} \\
0.10 & \omega = \mode{QLCS}  \\
0.05 & \omega = \mode{MCS}
\end{cases}
\]
Observed outcome: $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \mode{SUPER}$.

\paragraph{Scenario B: Hedged-correct \mode{MCS} forecast.}

The environment contains competing signals: strong large-scale forcing
favours organised convection, but whether the convection organises into a
quasi-linear convective system or a full mesoscale convective system
depends on cold-pool dynamics and low-level jet timing.  Faced with this
ambiguity, the system hedges across the organised modes with \mode{MCS}
as the possibilistic peak, while assigning non-trivial possibility to
\mode{QLCS} and \mode{SUPER}.  Subnormality is high ($m = 0.55$,
$\ign = 0.45$), explicitly advertising the system's limited confidence.

\[
\pi_B(\omega) = \begin{cases}
0.05 & \omega = \mode{NULL}  \\
0.10 & \omega = \mode{CELL}  \\
0.20 & \omega = \mode{SUPER} \\
0.40 & \omega = \mode{QLCS}  \\
0.55 & \omega = \mode{MCS}
\end{cases}
\]
Observed outcome: $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \mode{MCS}$.

\paragraph{Scenario C: Sharp-wrong \mode{NULL} forecast.}

On the surface this appears a quiescent day: large-scale forcing is weak,
instability is limited, and model guidance is unanimous for no organised
convection at the point of interest.  The system confidently assigns high
possibility to \mode{NULL} and near-zero to all convective categories.
In reality, an unresolved mesoscale boundary triggers unexpected
supercells---a mode the system deemed essentially impossible.

\[
\pi_C(\omega) = \begin{cases}
0.80 & \omega = \mode{NULL}  \\
0.10 & \omega = \mode{CELL}  \\
0.05 & \omega = \mode{SUPER} \\
0.00 & \omega = \mode{QLCS}  \\
0.00 & \omega = \mode{MCS}
\end{cases}
\]
Observed outcome: $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \mode{SUPER}$.

\subsection{Filling the Gauge}\label{sec:filling_gauge}

The gauge visualization maps each convective mode category to a horizontal
bar whose fill level represents raw possibility $\pi(\omega)$.  A fully
filled bar ($\pi = 1.0$) would indicate complete plausibility; the gap
between the tallest bar and $1.0$ is the ignorance $\ign$, rendered as
empty space to the right of every bar.  The observed category is
highlighted in green so that the reader can immediately judge whether
the system placed high possibility on the outcome that actually
occurred.  This representation makes subnormality and its consequences
visible at a glance, without requiring the reader to parse numerical
tables.

Figure~\ref{fig:filling_gauge} shows the gauge visualization for
Scenario~A.  The horizontal bars are filled to the raw possibility
level; the gap from $\Pi_{\max}$ to $1.0$ visually encodes the
ignorance $\ign$.  The observed category (\mode{SUPER}) is highlighted
in green.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{filling_gauge.png}
\caption{Gauge visualization for Scenario~A (sharp-correct \mode{SUPER}).
    Each horizontal bar represents a convective mode category, filled to
    its raw possibility value $\pi(\omega)$.  The vertical dashed line
    marks $\Pi_{\max} = 0.85$; the gap to $1.0$ is ignorance
    $\ign = 0.15$.  The observed category (\mode{SUPER}, green)
    dominates the distribution, confirming high conditional necessity.}
\label{fig:filling_gauge}
\end{figure}

Were we to draw the same gauge for Scenario~B, the picture would change
qualitatively.  Four bars would be partially filled, with \mode{MCS} the
tallest at only $0.55$; the bars for \mode{QLCS} ($0.40$) and
\mode{SUPER} ($0.20$) would reach substantial height as well.  The large
$\ign = 0.45$ gap would dominate the right-hand side of every bar,
immediately signaling that the system is hedging rather than committing.
The observed category (\mode{MCS}, green) would still be the tallest bar,
but only marginally so---consistent with a forecast that is directionally
correct yet openly uncertain.

Scenario~C would present the most visually striking gauge.  A single bar
(\mode{NULL}) would dominate at $0.80$, with \mode{CELL} contributing a
sliver at $0.10$ and the remaining three categories nearly empty.
The ignorance gap ($\ign = 0.20$) would be small, reinforcing the
impression of a confident forecast.  Yet the observed category
(\mode{SUPER}, green) would appear as a barely visible bar at
$0.05$---a stark visual indictment.  Placing the three gauges side by
side reveals at a glance that sharpness alone is not a virtue: it must
be aimed at the correct outcome.

Scenario~A represents the ideal case.  The depth-of-truth
$\alpha^* = 1.00$ confirms that the observed category (\mode{SUPER})
received the highest raw possibility after normalization, and the
conditional necessity of truth $\Nc^* = 0.882$ indicates that
alternatives were strongly down-weighted.  Ignorance is low
($\ign = 0.15$), nonspecificity is modest ($\eta = 0.247$), and the
resolution gap $\delta = +0.753$ is strongly positive---the system knew
what was happening and said so confidently.  Every metric in the
scorecard rewards this forecast.

Scenario~B tells a different but equally honest story.  The
depth-of-truth remains perfect ($\alpha^* = 1.00$) because \mode{MCS},
the observed category, is indeed the possibilistic peak after shape
normalization.  However, the system's hedging shows up clearly:
$\ign = 0.45$ is three times that of Scenario~A, nonspecificity
rises to $\eta = 0.473$, and $\Nc^* = 0.273$ is low because
\mode{QLCS} and \mode{SUPER} were assigned plausible alternatives.  The
resolution gap $\delta = +0.527$ is positive but moderate, reflecting
genuine skill tempered by acknowledged uncertainty.  This is the
signature of a well-calibrated system on a difficult day.

Scenario~C is the pathological case.  The system assigned
$\pi(\mode{SUPER}) = 0.05$---barely above zero---so the observed
outcome was deemed essentially impossible;
accordingly $\alpha^* = 0.063$ and $\Nc^* = 0.000$.  The resolution gap
$\delta = -0.175$ is the only negative value in the table, indicating
that the forecast pointed \emph{away} from the truth.  Critically,
ignorance was low ($\ign = 0.20$): the system was not hedging
cautiously---it was confident and wrong.  Low ignorance amplifies the
penalty because there is no uncertainty buffer to absorb the miss.

Contrasting Scenarios~B and~C reveals the scorecard's diagnostic power.
Both forecasts have noteworthy ignorance levels ($0.45$ and $0.20$,
respectively), yet the scorecard sharply distinguishes them.  Scenario~B
earns $\delta = +0.527$ because its hedging was aimed in the right
direction; Scenario~C earns $\delta = -0.175$ because its confidence
was aimed in the wrong direction.  A single summary statistic (e.g.,
mean possibility or maximum value) would conflate these two situations.
The five-number scorecard correctly separates ``honestly uncertain'' from
``confidently wrong,'' which is precisely the distinction that
operational forecasters need when tuning a possibilistic system.

\subsection{Pignistic Bridge Walkthrough}\label{sec:bridge_walkthrough}

We now apply the possibility-to-probability bridge
(Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}) to each scenario, then compute
information gain relative to a uniform climatological baseline
$p_{\mathrm{clim}} = 1/5 = 0.200$ per category
(surprise $= \log_2 5 = 2.322$~bits).
The bridge reserves $p_{\mathrm{ign}} = \ign$, distributes
the remaining $m$ proportionally via
$p_i = \pi_i \cdot m / \sum_j \pi_j$,
and appends the ignorance outcome.
Table~\ref{tab:bridge_ils} collects the results; the key pattern is
readable column by column.

    \textbf{Bottom:} ILS for the threshold event
    $A_T = \{\mode{SUPER},\, \mode{QLCS},\, \mode{MCS}\}$
    (organised convection).
    Asterisk ($^*$) denotes $\varepsilon$-floored values.}
\label{tab:bridge_ils}
\footnotesize
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}

The three scenarios trace a clear arc from skill to catastrophe.
Scenario~A's bridge concentrates probability on the correct outcome
($p = 0.688$); low ignorance means little mass is stranded in the
ignorance bin, yielding $\mathrm{IG} = +1.78$~bits and a tight
ILS interval $[0.80,\, 0.85]$.
Scenario~B is positive but modest ($\mathrm{IG} = +0.22$~bits): the
high $\ign = 0.45$ correctly deflates $p(\mode{MCS})$ from the
simple-normalization value of $0.55/1.30 = 0.423$ down to $0.233$,
honestly reflecting the system's uncertainty; the ILS interval
$[0.45,\, 0.55]$ confirms the ambivalence, though the event remains
more likely than not.
Scenario~C incurs steep penalties on both scores because the
bridge assigns only $p = 0.042$ to the observed outcome.  Crucially,
ignorance was modest ($\ign = 0.20$): the system was not hedging
cautiously---it was confident and wrong.  The wide ILS interval
$[0.00,\, 0.05]$ for organised convection confirms that the system
all but ruled out the event class that materialised.
