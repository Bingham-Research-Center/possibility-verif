\section{Worked Examples}\label{sec:worked_examples}

This section applies the three verification lanes to a trio of stylised
convective outlook scenarios: a sharp forecast that verifies correctly,
a hedged forecast that also verifies correctly, and a sharp forecast
that misses badly.  The walkthroughs proceed from categorical mode
verification through the probability bridge to the native possibilistic
scorecard, demonstrating how each lane adds diagnostic information
invisible to the preceding one.

%----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Scenario Setup}\label{sec:scenario_setup}

Three stylised possibilistic convective outlooks are defined, each
representing a distinct forecasting situation.  All distributions are
defined over $\Omega = \{\spc{NONE},\, \spc{MRGL},\, \spc{SLGT},\,
\spc{ENH},\, \spc{MDT},\, \spc{HIGH}\}$ with $K = 6$.  The possibilistic forecast is envisioned as an early or unofficial Day-1 outlook,
produced offline in parallel with real operations.  It is verified
against the final (pre-initiation) Day-1 SPC categorical outlook at
Norman, Oklahoma.  This like-for-like comparison has several advantages:
(a)~both forecasts share the same categorical universe $\Omega$;
(b)~information gain naturally measures the uncertainty reduction from
baseline to forecast; and (c)~the final outlook plays the ``truth''
role without requiring post-hoc mapping of storm reports to SPC
contours, which introduces its own ambiguities.

\paragraph{Scenario A: Sharp-correct \spc{MDT} forecast.}

A textbook severe weather environment is in place: surface-based CAPE
exceeds 3000~J~kg$^{-1}$, 0--6~km bulk shear surpasses 40~kt, and a
well-defined dryline provides a reliable mesoscale trigger.  The
ingredients for significant severe thunderstorms are all present and
co-located, so the system assigns high possibility to \spc{MDT} while
leaving only token values for neighbouring categories.  Subnormality is
low ($m = 0.90$, $\ign = 0.10$): the system is nearly fully committed.

\[
\pi_A(\omega) = \begin{cases}
0.00 & \omega = \spc{NONE} \\
0.00 & \omega = \spc{MRGL} \\
0.05 & \omega = \spc{SLGT} \\
0.15 & \omega = \spc{ENH}  \\
0.90 & \omega = \spc{MDT}  \\
0.10 & \omega = \spc{HIGH}
\end{cases}
\]
Observed outcome: $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{MDT}$.

\paragraph{Scenario B: Hedged-correct \spc{ENH} forecast.}

The environment contains competing signals: moderate CAPE
(1500--2000~J~kg$^{-1}$) and adequate deep-layer shear, but the timing
and location of the mesoscale trigger remain uncertain.  Whether
convection organises into discrete supercells or a mesoscale convective
system will determine whether the day verifies at \spc{SLGT},
\spc{ENH}, or even \spc{MDT}.  Faced with this ambiguity, the system
hedges: possibility is spread across four categories, and subnormality is
high ($m = 0.55$, $\ign = 0.45$), explicitly advertising the system's
limited confidence.

\[
\pi_B(\omega) = \begin{cases}
0.10 & \omega = \spc{NONE} \\
0.10 & \omega = \spc{MRGL} \\
0.40 & \omega = \spc{SLGT} \\
0.55 & \omega = \spc{ENH}  \\
0.30 & \omega = \spc{MDT}  \\
0.00 & \omega = \spc{HIGH}
\end{cases}
\]
Observed outcome: $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{ENH}$.

\paragraph{Scenario C: Sharp-wrong \spc{NONE} forecast.}

On the surface this appears a quiescent day: large-scale forcing is weak,
surface-based CAPE is marginal (less than 1000~J~kg$^{-1}$), and model
guidance unanimously favours no severe weather at the point of interest.
The system confidently assigns high possibility to \spc{NONE}---the true
null---and near-zero to all severe categories.  In reality, an unresolved
mesoscale convergence boundary interacts with the residual instability,
triggering an unexpected severe outbreak that verifies at \spc{MDT}---a
category the system deemed essentially impossible.

\[
\pi_C(\omega) = \begin{cases}
0.85 & \omega = \spc{NONE} \\
0.10 & \omega = \spc{MRGL} \\
0.05 & \omega = \spc{SLGT} \\
0.00 & \omega = \spc{ENH}  \\
0.00 & \omega = \spc{MDT}  \\
0.00 & \omega = \spc{HIGH}
\end{cases}
\]
Observed outcome: $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{MDT}$.

\subsection{Categorical Example}\label{sec:categorical_walkthrough}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{categorical_scores.png}
\caption{Categorical-lane verification for the three-year synthetic
    reforecast.
    \textbf{(a)}~Threshold performance: POD (purple), success ratio
    $1 - \text{FAR}$ (green), and CSI (dashed grey) at each severity
    threshold.  Scenarios~A and~B (green labels) contribute hits at
    their respective thresholds; Scenario~C (red cross) registers
    POD~$= 0$ at all thresholds.  HSS is computed from the full
    $K \times K$ table.
    \textbf{(b)}~Confusion matrix of forecast mode vs.\ observed
    category.  Green boxes mark the diagonal (correct mode); counts
    are on a log scale for visibility.  Off-diagonal mass clusters
    within one category of the diagonal, indicating near-miss errors.}
\label{fig:categorical_scores}
\end{figure*}

The categorical lane evaluates the mode-based point forecast:
$\hat{c} = \arg\max_\omega \pi(\omega)$, with ties broken by severity
(the higher category wins).  For each threshold
$t \in \{\text{\spc{MRGL}+},\, \text{\spc{SLGT}+},\,
\text{\spc{ENH}+},\, \text{\spc{MDT}+},\, \text{\spc{HIGH}}\}$,
the forecast issues ``yes'' if $\hat{c} \geq t$ and ``no'' otherwise;
the observation is ``yes'' if $c_{\mathrm{obs}} \geq t$.

Scenario~A: $\hat{c} = \spc{MDT}$, $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{MDT}$.
The mode is correct: hits at all thresholds up to \spc{MDT}+, miss only
at \spc{HIGH}.  POD $= 1.0$ for \spc{MRGL}+ through \spc{MDT}+;
FAR $= 0$ at those thresholds.

Scenario~B: $\hat{c} = \spc{ENH}$, $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{ENH}$.
Again correct: hits at \spc{MRGL}+ through \spc{ENH}+, correct
rejections at \spc{MDT}+ and \spc{HIGH}.

Scenario~C: $\hat{c} = \spc{NONE}$, $c_{\mathrm{obs}} = \spc{MDT}$.
The mode misses at every threshold---a complete failure of the
categorical forecast.  All thresholds register a miss (forecast ``no,''
observed ``yes''), yielding POD $= 0$ across the board.  The categorical
lane diagnoses this as starkly as the scorecard ($\alpha^* = 0$), but
without requiring any distributional machinery.

Figure~\ref{fig:categorical_scores} places these case studies in the
context of the full three-year synthetic reforecast.  Panel~(a) shows
how POD, success ratio ($1 - \text{FAR}$), and CSI degrade from
\spc{MRGL}+ to \spc{HIGH} as the threshold rises---a pattern familiar
from SPC verification, but here derived entirely from the possibilistic
mode.  Scenario~C's red cross at POD~$= 0$ is the visual counterpart
of the scorecard's $\alpha^* = 0$.  Panel~(b) reveals the error
structure: off-diagonal counts cluster near the main diagonal,
confirming that mode errors are predominantly near-miss rather than
catastrophic.

\subsection{Probabilistic Example}\label{sec:bridge_walkthrough}

Recall that the bridge reserves the ignorance fraction $\ign$ as an
explicit $(n{+}1)$-th probability outcome, distributing the remaining
mass proportionally among the original categories
(Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}).

The bridge is now applied to each scenario.  Information gain is then
computed relative to the SPC climatological baseline
$p_{\mathrm{clim}} = (0.60,\, 0.18,\, 0.12,\, 0.06,\, 0.032,\,
0.008)$, in which \spc{NONE} dominates and \spc{HIGH} is rare.
The bridge reserves $p_{\mathrm{ign}} = \ign$, distributes
the remaining $m$ proportionally via
$p_i = \pi_i \cdot m / \sum_j \pi_j$,
and appends the ignorance outcome.
Table~\ref{tab:bridge_ils} collects the results; the key pattern is
readable column by column.

\centering

    scenarios, relative to the SPC climatological baseline.
    Asterisk ($^*$) denotes $\varepsilon$-floored values
    ($\varepsilon = 0.01$; see text).}
\label{tab:bridge_ils}
\footnotesize
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
 & \textbf{A} (sharp- & \textbf{B} (hedged- & \textbf{C} (sharp- \\
 & correct) & correct) & wrong) \\
\midrule
$\ign$ & $0.10$ & $0.45$ & $0.15$ \\
$\sum \pi$ & $1.20$ & $1.45$ & $1.00$ \\
$p(c_{\mathrm{obs}})$ & $0.6750$ & $0.2086$ & $0.0100^*$ \\
$p_{\mathrm{clim}}(c_{\mathrm{obs}})$ & $0.0320$ & $0.0600$ & $0.0320$ \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The three scenarios trace a clear arc from skill to catastrophe.
Scenario~A's bridge concentrates probability on the correct outcome
($p = 0.675$); low ignorance means little mass is stranded in the
ignorance bin, yielding $\mathrm{IG} = +4.399$~bits---strong
skill relative to the climatological baseline, which assigns only
$3.2\%$ probability to \spc{MDT}.
Scenario~B is positive and substantial ($\mathrm{IG} = +1.798$~bits):
the high $\ign = 0.45$ correctly deflates $p(\spc{ENH})$ from the
simple-normalisation value of $0.55/1.45 = 0.379$ down to $0.209$,
honestly reflecting the system's uncertainty, but the forecast still
beats the $6\%$ climatological rate for \spc{ENH} by a wide margin.
Scenario~C incurs a penalty ($\mathrm{IG} = -1.678$~bits) because the
bridge assigns zero probability to the observed outcome.  The
$\varepsilon$-floor ($\varepsilon = 0.01$) prevents an infinite
penalty but still yields a surprise of $6.644$~bits---far worse than
the climatological surprise of $4.966$~bits for \spc{MDT}.  Crucially,
low ignorance ($\ign = 0.15$) provides almost no cushion: the system
left itself no escape route for an outcome it had ruled out.

The $\varepsilon$-floor is set at $0.01$, representing the quantisation
floor for a system built on $O(10)$ ensemble members and human
post-processing.  Sensitivity analysis confirms the qualitative
conclusion is invariant: $\varepsilon = 0.001$ yields $9.97$~bits
surprise; $\varepsilon = 0.0001$ yields $13.29$~bits.  In all cases
Scenario~C remains strongly negative.

\subsection{Full Scorecard Comparison}\label{sec:scorecard_comparison}

Before turning to the five-number scorecard, Figure~\ref{fig:filling_gauge}
introduces a gauge visualisation for Scenario~A: each SPC category maps
to a horizontal bar filled to its raw possibility $\pi(\omega)$, with
the gap from $\Pi_{\max}$ to $1.0$ encoding ignorance $\ign$.  The
observed category is highlighted in green.  For Scenario~B the gauge
would show five partially filled bars with a large $\ign = 0.45$ gap,
immediately signalling hedging; for Scenario~C a single dominant bar
(\spc{NONE} at $0.85$) with the observed category (\spc{MDT}) unfilled
at zero---sharpness aimed at the wrong outcome.

Scenario~A represents the ideal case.  The depth-of-truth
$\alpha^* = 1.00$ confirms that the observed category (\spc{MDT})
received the highest raw possibility after normalisation, and the
conditional necessity of truth $\Nc^* = 0.833$ indicates that
alternatives were strongly down-weighted.  Ignorance is low
($\ign = 0.10$), nonspecificity is modest ($\eta = 0.222$), and the
resolution gap $\delta = +0.778$ is strongly positive---the system knew
what was happening and said so confidently.  Every metric in the
scorecard rewards this forecast.

Scenario~B tells a different but equally honest story.  The
depth-of-truth remains perfect ($\alpha^* = 1.00$) because \spc{ENH},
the observed category, is indeed the possibilistic peak after shape
normalisation.  However, the system's hedging shows up clearly:
$\ign = 0.45$ is nearly five times that of Scenario~A, nonspecificity
rises to $\eta = 0.439$, and $\Nc^* = 0.273$ is low because
\spc{SLGT} and \spc{MDT} were assigned plausible alternatives.  The
resolution gap $\delta = +0.561$ is positive but moderate, reflecting
genuine skill tempered by acknowledged uncertainty.  This is the
signature of a well-calibrated system on a difficult day.

Scenario~C is the pathological case.  The system assigned
$\pi(\spc{MDT}) = 0.00$, declaring the observed outcome impossible;
accordingly $\alpha^* = 0.00$ and $\Nc^* = 0.00$.  The resolution gap
$\delta = -0.196$ is the only negative value in the table, indicating
that the forecast pointed \emph{away} from the truth.  Critically,
ignorance was low ($\ign = 0.15$): the system was not hedging
cautiously---it was confident and wrong.  Low ignorance amplifies the
penalty because there is no uncertainty buffer to absorb the miss.

Contrasting Scenarios~B and~C reveals the scorecard's diagnostic power.
Both forecasts have noteworthy ignorance levels ($0.45$ and $0.15$,
respectively), yet the scorecard sharply distinguishes them.  Scenario~B
earns $\delta = +0.561$ because its hedging was aimed in the right
direction; Scenario~C earns $\delta = -0.196$ because its confidence
was aimed in the wrong direction.  A single summary statistic (e.g.,
mean possibility or maximum value) would conflate these two situations.
The five-number scorecard correctly separates ``honestly uncertain'' from
``confidently wrong,'' which is precisely the distinction that
operational forecasters need when tuning a possibilistic system.

\textbf{Why five numbers?}  Three diagnostic components
(Table~\ref{tab:three_to_five}) expand to five post-event metrics
because the possibility component requires three numbers to capture
support for the truth ($\alpha^*$), forecast spread ($\eta$), and their
net balance ($\delta$).  Section~\ref{sec:tripartite_value} provides the
formal justification via hypothesis tests; the three scenarios make the
complementarity tangible:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Truth-finding pair} ($\alpha^*$ vs.\ $\Nc^*$).
    Scenario~B has $\alpha^* = 1.00$ but $\Nc^* = 0.273$: truth was the
    top pick but closely contested by alternatives.  Had the observed
    category been \spc{ENH} instead of \spc{MDT} in Scenario~A's
    distribution, $\alpha^*$ would drop to $0.167$ while $\Nc^*$ would
    remain zero---truth was plausible but far from dominant.
    $\alpha^*$ answers ``how much support did the truth receive?'';
    $\Nc^*$ answers ``was the truth \textit{the} winner?''
\item \textbf{Uncertainty pair} ($\eta$ vs.\ $\ign$).
    Scenario~B has $\eta = 0.439$ and $\ign = 0.45$: the system was both
    hedged in shape and uncertain in commitment.  Scenario~C has
    $\eta = 0.196$ and $\ign = 0.15$: sharp in shape \textit{and}
    confident in commitment---but aimed at the wrong answer.
    $\eta$ measures shape spread (normalised); $\ign$ measures overall
    confidence (raw).  They can move independently.
\item \textbf{Net resolution} ($\delta$).
    The one number that combines truth-finding with spread: $\delta > 0$
    means the forecast's shape helped; $\delta < 0$ means it hurt.
    No other single metric captures this.
\end{itemize}
A reduced scorecard would conflate diagnostically distinct situations:
dropping $\Nc^*$ loses the ``winner vs.\ supported'' distinction;
dropping $\ign$ hides the confidence axis; dropping $\delta$ obscures
whether the shape pointed toward or away from the truth.
