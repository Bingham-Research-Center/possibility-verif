\subsection{Structured Uncertainty and LLM-Mediated Communication}%
\label{sec:llm_communication}

The three-component representation ($\poss$, $\ign$, $\Nc$) was designed
not only for verification but also for downstream communication.  In a
companion system, a large language model (LLM) ingests the structured
triplet and generates plain-language advisories at multiple complexity
levels---public, operational, and technical---with each statement grounded
in specific forecast quantities.  The explicit ignorance signal is
critical: it gives the LLM a principled basis for expressing uncertainty
(``confidence is limited today; conditions are unusual'') rather than
manufacturing false precision from a normalised distribution.
Conditional necessity provides the complementary trigger for confident
statements (``\spc{MDT} risk dominates all alternatives'').  The
verification framework developed here closes the loop: the five-number
scorecard evaluates whether the structured output that feeds
communication is itself trustworthy.  Details of the LLM translation
layer are beyond the present scope; the point relevant to verification is
that possibilistic forecasts produce machine-readable uncertainty
metadata that probabilistic point forecasts do not.
