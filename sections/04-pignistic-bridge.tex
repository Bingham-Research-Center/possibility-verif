\section{Converting Possibilities to Probabilities}%
\label{sec:pignistic_bridge}

Standard verification scores---the logarithmic score, Brier score
\citep{Brier1950-lc}, and information gain---require probability inputs
that sum to unity.  A possibility distribution does not satisfy this
constraint: its values encode plausibility rather than relative
frequency, and a subnormal distribution does not even reach a maximum of
one.  The conversion developed below transforms a subnormal possibility
distribution into an $(n{+}1)$-category probability vector, preserving
the subnormality signal as an explicit ignorance outcome rather than
erasing it through na\"ive normalisation.  The term \textit{pignistic}
(from Latin \textit{pignus}, a wager) denotes the probabilities one
would assign if forced to bet; the conversion shares this forced-betting
intuition.

%----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Possibility-to-Probability Conversion}%
\label{sec:poss_prob_bridge}

Applying probabilistic verification to possibilistic forecasts requires
converting possibility distributions to probabilities.  The conversion
preserves the subnormality signal through three steps:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Reserve ignorance mass:}
    $p_{\mathrm{ign}} = \ign = 1 - \max(\pi)$.
\item \textbf{Distribute the remaining $(1 - \ign)$ proportionally
    across outcomes:}
    \[
    p_i = \frac{\pi_i \cdot (1 - \ign)}{\sum_j \pi_j}
    \]
\item \textbf{Append $p_{\mathrm{ign}}$ as an explicit ``ignorance
    outcome,''} yielding a probability distribution over $n+1$
    categories that sums to unity.
\end{enumerate}

Simple normalisation ($p_i = \pi_i / \sum_j \pi_j$) erases
subnormality---probabilities always sum to one regardless of model
confidence.  This is structurally analogous to the pignistic
transformation of \citet{Smets1990-MISSING}, which was designed for
normal belief functions.  The conversion preserves the ignorance signal,
ensuring that verification scores correctly penalise uncertain forecasts
by routing probability mass into a non-observed outcome.  For normal
distributions ($\max \pi = 1$, $\ign = 0$), both methods coincide.
The key innovation over the pignistic transform is reserving the
ignorance mass as an explicit outcome rather than redistributing it,
enabling verification scores to penalise subnormality directly.

\begin{workedexample}[Worked example]
Suppose a possibilistic convective outlook produces
$\pi = (0.05,\; 0.2,\; 0.4,\; 0.6,\; 0.1,\; 0.0)$.
\begin{align*}
\textit{Input:}\quad
  & \ign = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4,\quad
    \textstyle\sum_j \pi_j = 1.35 \\
  & \quad (\text{40\% of plausibility budget unassigned}) \\[4pt]
\textit{Output:}\quad
  & \mathbf{p} = (0.022,\; 0.089,\; 0.178,\; 0.267, \\
  & \qquad 0.044,\; 0.0,\;
    \underbrace{0.400}_{\text{ign}}) \\[4pt]
\textit{Surprise:}\quad
  & \text{If \spc{ENH} obs: }
    S = {-}\log_2(0.267) = 1.91\;\text{bits} \\
  & S_{\mathrm{clim}} = {-}\log_2(0.06) = 4.06\;\text{bits} \\[4pt]
\textit{IG:}\quad
  & 4.06 - 1.91 = 2.15\;\text{bits gained}
\end{align*}

High \ign{} ($0.4$) correctly inflated the ignorance outcome, reducing
$p(\spc{ENH})$ from what simple normalisation would give
($0.6/1.35 = 0.444$) to $0.267$.  The forecast surprise ($1.91$~bits)
honestly reflects the system's partial uncertainty.
Figure~\ref{fig:pignistic_bridge} visualises this transformation.
\end{workedexample}

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{pignistic_bridge.png}
\caption{Possibility-to-probability conversion applied to a subnormal
    distribution.  \textbf{Left:} raw possibility values $\pi(\omega)$

\subsection{Information-Gain Decomposition}\label{sec:ig_decomposition}

Once possibilities have been converted to probabilities, standard
verification applies.  Define the \textbf{surprise} of a forecast at the
observed outcome as the log score:
\begin{equation}
S(f,\, c_{\mathrm{obs}})
    = -\log_2 p_f(c_{\mathrm{obs}}) \quad \text{(bits)}
\label{eq:surprise}
\end{equation}
Low surprise means the forecast assigned high probability to what
actually happened; the logarithm makes the score particularly sensitive
to confident misses on rare events.

\textbf{Information gain} (IG) measures the reduction in surprise
from replacing a baseline forecast $f_1$ (e.g., climatology) with the
possibilistic forecast $f_2$ (after conversion):
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IG}
    = S(f_1,\, c_{\mathrm{obs}})
    - S(f_2,\, c_{\mathrm{obs}})
\label{eq:ig}
\end{equation}
Positive IG indicates that the forecast reduced surprise relative to
the baseline \citep{Roulston2002-eq}.  (Equivalently, IG is the
difference in sample Kullback--Leibler divergences from the observation
to each forecast; \citet{Lawson2024-bu} extended this to atmospheric
forecast evaluation with explicit decomposition diagnostics.)

Averaging Eq.~\eqref{eq:ig} over a verification sample, the mean
surprise decomposes into three diagnostic terms:
\begin{equation}
\overline{S}
    = \mathrm{UNC} - \mathrm{DSC} + \mathrm{REL}
\quad \text{(all in bits; lower is better)}
\label{eq:ig_decomp}
\end{equation}
where \textbf{UNC} is the irreducible uncertainty (entropy of the base
rate), \textbf{DSC} is discrimination (can the system tell events
apart?\ higher is better), and \textbf{REL} is calibration error (are
stated probabilities honest?\ lower is better).  We use DSC
(discrimination) rather than the conventional RES (resolution) to avoid
confusion with horizontal grid resolution in
NWP.\footnote{DSC here denotes the same quantity as RES (resolution) in
the Brier score decomposition.  It should not be confused with the
distinct ``discrimination'' concept of \citet{Murphy1993-MISSING}, which
conditions on observations rather than forecasts.}

When the baseline is climatology,
$\overline{S}(\mathrm{clim}) = \mathrm{UNC}$, and information gain
reduces to
$\mathrm{IG} = \mathrm{DSC} - \mathrm{REL}$: positive IG requires
discrimination to exceed calibration error.  This decomposition is the
logarithmic counterpart of the Brier score's three-component breakdown
($\mathrm{BS} = \mathrm{REL} - \mathrm{RES} + \mathrm{UNC}$) but
operates in bits---additive, interpretable information units.

Figure~\ref{fig:ig_decomp} illustrates the decomposition for several
forecast scenarios with net IG annotated.

\begin{figure}[!b]

\caption{Information-gain decomposition for five forecast archetypes
    (illustrative values).  Purple bars show gross discrimination

\end{figure}

\subsection{Why Na\"ive Normalisation Erases Information}%
\label{sec:naive_normalisation}

Simple normalisation ($p_i = \pi_i / \sum_j \pi_j$) is the standard
route from possibilities to probabilities.  It forces the result to sum
to one regardless of model confidence: a system with $\ign = 0.9$
(leaving 90\% of the unit-interval plausibility budget unassigned)
produces the same probability vector as one with $\ign = 0.1$,
provided their raw shapes are proportional.  The ignorance signal is erased.  Verification scores
applied to the normalised vector cannot distinguish a sharp, confident
forecast from a diffuse, hedged one---the answer to ``how would you
bet?'' is given without acknowledging ``I wouldn't bet at all.''

The conversion avoids this by deferring
probabilistic commitment for the ignorance portion.  Mass equal to
$\ign$ is routed into an $(n{+}1)$-th outcome that can never be the
observed category.  Uncertain forecasts are penalised because this
stranded mass earns no verification credit: it inflates the self-entropy
of the converted forecast in proportion to $\ign$.  The more ignorant
the system, the more mass is stranded, and the larger the penalty.

\begin{workedexample}[Worked contrast]
Using $\pi = (0.05,\, 0.2,\, 0.4,\allowbreak\, 0.6,\, 0.1,\, 0.0)$
from Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & Simple norm.\ & Conversion \\
\midrule
$p(\spc{ENH})$ & $0.6/1.35 = 0.444$ & $0.267$ \\
$p_{\mathrm{ign}}$ & --- & $0.400$ \\
Self-entropy (bits) & $-\log_2(0.444) = 1.17$ & $-\log_2(0.267) = 1.91$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The $0.74$-bit gap is the verification cost of ignoring the
system's admitted uncertainty.  Simple normalisation rewards the system
for confidence it never claimed.
\end{workedexample}

\textbf{Complementarity of \ign{} and IG.}
Possibilistic ignorance $\ign$ and information gain IG capture
complementary dimensions of forecast quality:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{$\ign$ (possibilistic ignorance):} Measures the
    forecasting system's support strength---did the system know what was
    going on?  Derived from the subnormality of the raw output
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:ignorance}).
\item \textbf{IG (information gain):} Measures forecast value---did the
    forecast reduce surprise about what happened?  Derived from the
    probability-converted forecast evaluated against observations.
\end{itemize}

Both are needed because:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item A \textbf{sharp wrong} forecast has low \ign{} but negative IG.
\item A \textbf{hedged correct} forecast has high \ign{} but positive IG.
\item A \textbf{sharp correct} forecast has low \ign{} and high IG---the
    ideal.
\item A \textbf{hedged wrong} forecast has high \ign{} and negative
    IG---the system knew it didn't know, and it still got it wrong.
\end{itemize}
