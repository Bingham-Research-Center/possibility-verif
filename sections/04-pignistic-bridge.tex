\section{Pignistic Bridge}\label{sec:pignistic_bridge}

The term \textit{pignistic} derives from the Latin \textit{pignus} (a
wager): it denotes the probabilities one would assign if forced to bet.
Standard verification scores---the logarithmic score, Brier score \citep{Brier1950-lc}, and
information gain---require probability inputs that sum to unity.  A
possibility distribution does not satisfy this constraint: its values
encode plausibility rather than relative frequency, and a subnormal
distribution does not even reach a maximum of one.  The
\textit{possibility-to-probability bridge} developed below converts a
subnormal possibility distribution into an $(n{+}1)$-category probability
vector, preserving the subnormality signal as an explicit ignorance
outcome rather than erasing it through na\"ive normalization.

%----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Possibility-to-Probability Bridge}\label{sec:poss_prob_bridge}

Applying probabilistic verification to possibilistic forecasts requires
converting possibility distributions to probabilities.  The
\textit{possibility-to-probability bridge} preserves the subnormality
signal through three steps:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Reserve ignorance mass:}
    $p_{\mathrm{ign}} = \ign = 1 - \max(\pi)$.
\item \textbf{Distribute the remaining $(1 - \ign)$ proportionally
    across outcomes:}
    \[
    p_i = \frac{\pi_i \cdot (1 - \ign)}{\sum_j \pi_j}
    \]
\item \textbf{Append $p_{\mathrm{ign}}$ as an explicit ``ignorance
    outcome,''} yielding a probability distribution over $n+1$
    categories that sums to unity.
\end{enumerate}

Simple normalization ($p_i = \pi_i / \sum_j \pi_j$) erases
subnormality---probabilities always sum to one regardless of model
confidence.  This is equivalent to the pignistic transformation of
\citet{Smets1990-MISSING}, which was designed for normal belief
functions.  The bridge preserves the ignorance signal,
ensuring that verification scores correctly penalize uncertain forecasts
by routing probability mass into a non-observed outcome.  For normal
distributions ($\max \pi = 1$, $\ign = 0$), both methods coincide.

This extension to subnormal distributions generalizes the pignistic transformation, which \citet{Smets1990-MISSING} developed within the transferable belief model for normal belief functions.  The key innovation is reserving the ignorance mass as an explicit outcome rather than redistributing it, enabling verification scores to penalize subnormality directly.

\textbf{Worked example.}
Suppose a possibilistic convective outlook produces
$\pi = (0.05,\; 0.2,\; 0.4,\; 0.6,\; 0.1,\; 0.0)$.
\begin{align*}
\textit{Input:}\quad
  & \ign = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4 \;\;(\text{40\% unsure}),
    \quad \textstyle\sum_j \pi_j = 1.35 \\
\textit{Bridge:}\quad
  & \mathbf{p} = (0.022,\; 0.089,\; 0.178,\; 0.267,\;
    0.044,\; 0.0,\; \underbrace{0.400}_{\text{ign}}) \\
\textit{Score:}\quad
  & \text{If \spc{ENH} obs: } {-}\log_2(0.267) = 1.91\;\text{bits};
    \quad p_{\mathrm{clim}}(\spc{ENH}) = 0.05 \Rightarrow 4.32\;\text{bits} \\
\textit{IG:}\quad
  & 4.32 - 1.91 = 2.41\;\text{bits gained over climatology}
\end{align*}

High \ign{} ($0.4$) correctly inflated the ignorance outcome, reducing
$p(\spc{ENH})$ from what simple normalization would give
($0.6/1.35 = 0.444$) to $0.267$.  The verification score ($1.85$~bits)
honestly reflects the system's partial uncertainty.
Figure~\ref{fig:pignistic_bridge} visualizes this transformation.

\caption{Possibility-to-probability bridge applied to a subnormal possibility

\subsection{Information-Gain Decomposition}\label{sec:ig_decomposition}

The logarithmic scoring rule---which assigns a penalty of $-\log_2 p(c_{\mathrm{obs}})$ bits for the probability assigned to the observed outcome---is the natural companion to the bridge.  Its information-theoretic decomposition into uncertainty, discrimination, and reliability mirrors the Brier score's three-component decomposition but operates in logarithmic units, making it particularly sensitive to confident misses on rare events.

After the bridge converts possibilities to probabilities,
standard information-theoretic verification applies.  Information gain
measures the reduction in surprise from replacing one forecast with
another:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IG} = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(o \,\|\, f_1) - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(o \,\|\, f_2)
\label{eq:ig}
\end{equation}
where $o$ is the observation, $f_1$ is the baseline (e.g., climatology),
and $f_2$ is the possibilistic forecast (after bridge conversion).
Positive IG indicates that the forecast reduced surprise relative to the
baseline.
This formulation follows \citet{Roulston2002-eq}; \citet{Lawson2024-bu} extended it to atmospheric forecast evaluation with explicit decomposition diagnostics.

The mean logarithmic score of the forecast decomposes diagnostically:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{LS} = \mathrm{UNC} - \mathrm{DSC} + \mathrm{REL}
\quad \text{(all in bits; lower is better)}
\label{eq:ig_decomp}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{UNC} is irreducible uncertainty (entropy of the base rate).
\item \textbf{REL} is calibration error (lower is better): are stated
    probabilities honest?
\item \textbf{DSC} is discrimination (higher is better): can the system
    tell events apart?
\end{itemize}

\noindent When the baseline $f_1$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ig} is climatology,
$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(o \,\|\, \mathrm{clim}) = \mathrm{UNC}$, and information
gain reduces to $\mathrm{IG} = \mathrm{DSC} - \mathrm{REL}$.  Positive IG
thus requires discrimination to exceed calibration error.

This decomposition diagnoses whether a possibilistic forecast's value
arises from calibration or from discriminating between severe-weather
regimes.  Figure~\ref{fig:ig_decomp} illustrates the decomposition for
several forecast scenarios with net IG annotated.

The IG decomposition is the logarithmic counterpart of the Brier score's familiar three-component breakdown ($\mathrm{BS} = \mathrm{REL} - \mathrm{RES} + \mathrm{UNC}$).  Where the Brier decomposition measures skill in probability-squared units, the IG decomposition measures it in bits---additive, interpretable information units.  A reliability diagram plots observed frequency against forecast probability; the REL term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ig_decomp} is its scalar summary.  Similarly, the DSC term summarizes the spread of conditional outcome distributions that a reliability diagram visualizes as deviations from the diagonal.

\begin{figure*}[!b]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{ig_decomposition.png}
\caption{Information-gain decomposition for five forecast scenarios.
    Bars show net $\mathrm{IG} = \mathrm{DSC} - \mathrm{REL}$ (bits),
    with the zero baseline representing climatological performance.
    Purple bars show discrimination (DSC; positive $=$ skill); green

\end{figure*}

To avoid naming collision with possibilistic ignorance $\ign$, this
manuscript uses ``information gain'' (IG) rather than the abbreviation
IGN used in some verification literature.

\subsection{Why Na\"ive Normalization Erases Information}%
\label{sec:naive_normalization}

Simple normalization ($p_i = \pi_i / \sum_j \pi_j$) is the standard
route from possibilities to probabilities.  It forces the result to sum
to one regardless of model confidence: a system that is $90\%$ unsure
($\ign = 0.9$) produces the same probability vector as one that is
$10\%$ unsure ($\ign = 0.1$), provided their raw shapes are
proportional.  The ignorance signal is erased.  Verification scores
applied to the normalized vector cannot distinguish a sharp, confident
forecast from a diffuse, hedged one---the answer to ``how would you
bet?'' is given without acknowledging ``I wouldn't bet at all.''

The possibility-to-probability bridge avoids this by deferring
probabilistic commitment for the ignorance portion.  Mass equal to
$\ign$ is routed into an $(n{+}1)$-th outcome that can never be the
observed category.  Uncertain forecasts are penalized because this
stranded mass earns no verification credit: it inflates the self-entropy
of the converted forecast in proportion to $\ign$.  The more ignorant
the system, the more mass is stranded, and the larger the penalty.

\textbf{Worked contrast.}
Using $\pi = (0.05,\, 0.2,\, 0.4,\allowbreak\, 0.6,\, 0.1,\, 0.0)$
from Section~\ref{sec:poss_prob_bridge}:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & Simple norm.\ & Bridge \\
\midrule
$p(\spc{ENH})$ & $0.6/1.35 = 0.444$ & $0.267$ \\
$p_{\mathrm{ign}}$ & --- & $0.400$ \\
Self-entropy (bits) & $-\log_2(0.444) = 1.17$ & $-\log_2(0.267) = 1.91$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The $0.74$-bit gap is the verification cost of ignoring the
system's admitted uncertainty.  Simple normalization rewards the system
for confidence it never claimed.

\textbf{Complementarity of \ign{} and IG.}
Possibilistic ignorance $\ign$ and information gain IG capture
complementary dimensions of forecast quality:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{$\ign$ (possibilistic ignorance):} Measures the
    forecasting system's support strength---did the system know what was
    going on?  Derived from the subnormality of the raw output
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:ignorance}).
\item \textbf{IG (information gain):} Measures forecast value---did the
    forecast reduce surprise about what happened?  Derived from the
    probability-converted forecast evaluated against observations.
\end{itemize}

Both are needed because:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item A \textbf{sharp wrong} forecast has low \ign{} but negative IG.
\item A \textbf{hedged correct} forecast has high \ign{} but positive IG.
\item A \textbf{sharp correct} forecast has low \ign{} and high IG---the
    ideal.
\item A \textbf{hedged wrong} forecast has high \ign{} and negative
    IG---the system knew it didn't know, and it still got it wrong.
\end{itemize}
