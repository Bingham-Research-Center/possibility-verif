% --- The "ignorance erasure" problem ---

% [PLACEHOLDER: 1–2 paragraphs.  Central argument:
%  - Classical possibility theory (Dubois & Prade 1988) requires
%    normalisation: max pi = 1.
%  - Normalising a subnormal distribution destroys the ignorance signal,
%    inflating confidence in forecasts the system cannot support.
%  - This is analogous to forcing an ensemble whose members all have low
%    confidence to produce a calibrated probability that sums to unity —
%    the "ignorance" is absorbed into the distribution rather than flagged.
%  - We call this "ignorance erasure" and argue it is the central obstacle

% --- Paper outline ---

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:poss_primer} introduces the mathematical foundations of
possibility theory, emphasising subnormal distributions and the
three-component uncertainty framework.
Section~\ref{sec:pignistic_bridge} develops the tripartite pignistic
bridge that converts possibilities to probabilities without erasing
ignorance, and reviews the information-gain decomposition.
Section~\ref{sec:native_verification} formalises native possibilistic
verification: the five-number scorecard, interval log score, imprecise
ranked logarithmic score, and possibilistic climatology.
Section~\ref{sec:tripartite_value} establishes three hypothesis tests
demonstrating that each uncertainty component (possibility, ignorance,
conditional necessity) independently contributes verification value.
Section~\ref{sec:worked_examples} walks through three worked examples
using SPC convective outlook categories (\spc{MRGL} through \spc{HIGH}).
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses connections to Dempster--Shafer
theory, applicability beyond severe weather, and future directions.
